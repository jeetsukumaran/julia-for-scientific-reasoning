0.0.1 Dice pools

Concepts
A die: a physical randomization device

A “die” (singular; plural is “dice”) is a device used for generating random outcomes in a finite discrete sample space. As a physical object, a die typically corresponds to some device that has a fixed, finite number of distinguishable “sides”, each of which can be mapped unambiguously, if not always uniquely, to a “face”, representing a unique and mutually-exclusive outcome in a fixed sample space . Dice are often marked with symbols to allow for identification of sides with faces. As in many modern dice the outcomes are indexed numerically, representing samples from a subset of the natural numbers, the symbols on these faces are numbers indicating integer values \({1, 2, \dots, N}\), where \(N\) is the number of faces.

Example: A 6-sided die

A type of die most of us are familiar with today is a 6-sided die 1: 🎲. Here, the die has six sides, and each side is marked with a distinct symbol to allow it to unique represent a distinct face in the sample space 2. Today, very often, the faces are represented by a subset of the natural numbers \(\mathcal{S} \subset \mathrm{N}\), i.e., \(\mathcal{S} = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}\), though other symbols have and continue to be used (e.g. …).

Generating an outcome

A die is “cast” 3 , “thrown”, or “rolled” to generate an outcome from its sample space, (usually) with the face on the side aligned upwards after it stops moving representing the realized outcome.

Generalized polyhedral dice to sample from \(\mathrm{N}\).

While the six-sided cuboid die is most familiar to most people today, dice types vary in the number of sides, such as two-sided, four-sided, ten-sided, twenty-sided etc. dice, corresponding to sample spaces with corresponding varying numbers of elementary outcomes (2, 4, 10, 20, etc.)

A standard set of such six such generalized polyhedral dice types are found across a broad range of games and recreational contexts, consisting of: (1) a four-sided die, (2) a six-sided die, (3) an eight-sided die, (4) a ten-sided die, (5) a twelve-sided die, and (6) a twenty-sided die. For convenience, we may use the dice notation used by these game communities to reference specific types of these dice: d4, d6, d8, d10, d12, and d20, respectively.

In each of these dice, each of its sides maps to a unique integer from 1 to the number of sides. Thus an \(N\)-sided die can be seen as having a sample space consisting of \(\{1, 2, \dots N\}\). This mapping to natural numbers allows construction of more complex sample spaces by allowing for combining outcomes from multiple dice using natural number algebra such as addition.

Most of us are accustomed to the concept of the familiar cuboid six-sided die used in this way, e.g., when rolling two, three, or more of these and taking the total of the individual values of the roll as the outcome. A dice pool, discussed next, is a generalization of this concept.

Dice pools

We can model a dice pool as a multiset 4 of dice that is constructed by sampling with replacement from a set of candidate dice types. For example, there are many games in which various different moves and plays are effected by sampling from a dice pool made up of two units of single type of die (six-sided).

4 A multiset is a collection of distinguishable elements with, unlike sets, allowance for repeats.

Dice pool notation

We can describe the configuration of a dice pool using dice notation, which enumerates all the dice used by the mathematical conventions of indicating products (within types) and additions (across types) to combine them.

Probabilistics
Probability space
Probability space of a die with \(N\) sides

Each side of each of these dice is indexed uniquely indexed with an integer, starting from one and proceeding in sequence, ${1, 2, }, through each and every side of the dice (with some allowance for what constitutes a “side” due to the geometrical aspects of the physical manifestation of the die, e.g. a pyramid for a 4-sided die has the edges for “side” ). As such, an \(N-\)sided die, where \(N\) is some natural number greater than 1, \(N \in \mathbb{N}, N > 1\) has a sample space consisting of a subset of natural numbers, \(\mathcal{S} \subset \mathbb{N}, \mathcal{S} = \{1, 2, \dots, N\}\).

TipBrute force approach: ALWAYS works, IF you can do it

Here is a technique that will solve EVERY single probability calculation problem conceivable.

When asked what is the probability of some event,

  1. Count all the elementary outcomes.

  2. Count all elementary outcomes that are included in (or correspond to) the event.

Then divide the second by the first.
